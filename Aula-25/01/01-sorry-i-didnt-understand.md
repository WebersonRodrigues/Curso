# Texto 1 – Sorry, I Didn't Understand

## 📖 Texto – "Sorry, I Didn't Understand"

My name is Lucas, and today I'm video-calling my pen pal Jake from California. We talk every week, but sometimes it's hard to understand everything he says.

Jake starts the call with a big smile. "Hey Lucas! What's up, dude? That math test was totally a piece of cake!"

I look confused. **Sorry, I didn't catch that.** What's a "piece of cake"? Is he talking about food?

Jake laughs. "Oh, **let me rephrase that.** **What I meant was** the test was really easy. 'Piece of cake' means something is easy."

"**Oh, I see what you mean!** That's a funny expression. We don't say that in Portuguese."

Then Jake asks me about my weekend. I try to explain that I went to a "festa junina" at my school, but I say: "We danced with fire and ate corn on a stick."

Jake looks worried. "You danced WITH fire?!"

"**Sorry, that came out wrong.** **What I'm trying to say is** we danced NEAR a big bonfire, not with fire in our hands! **Let me explain** — it's a traditional party."

Jake relaxes. "Oh okay! **Does that make sense** now — like a campfire party?"

"Yes! **In other words**, it's like a country festival with food, music, and games."

Later, Jake tells me about his baseball game. He says: "I hit a grand slam yesterday!"

**I'm not sure I follow.** "**Could you say that differently?** I don't know baseball words."

Jake thinks for a moment. "**To put it simply**, I hit the ball really far and my whole team scored. It was amazing!"

"**Now I get it!** That sounds awesome!"

Then I try to tell Jake that my dog is "very smart" but I accidentally say "my dog is very handsome."

Jake laughs. "Your dog is handsome?"

"**I didn't mean it like that!** **I think there's been a misunderstanding.** **What I mean is** my dog learns tricks quickly. He's intelligent!"

"Oh! **In other words**, your dog is clever! That's cool!"

We both laugh. Even when we don't understand each other perfectly, we always figure it out. That's what friends do.

### 🧠 Estruturas gramaticais do texto

**1. "What I meant was…" / "What I'm trying to say is…" — reformulando o que você disse**

> What I meant was the test was really easy.  
> What I'm trying to say is we danced near a bonfire.

Estruturas para corrigir ou esclarecer algo que você disse:
- **What I meant was** + correção (passado — corrigindo algo que já disse)
- **What I'm trying to say is** + explicação (presente — tentando se expressar agora)
- **What I mean is** + esclarecimento (presente — explicando sua intenção)

Exemplos:
- What I meant was I like the color, not the shape. (O que eu quis dizer foi que gosto da cor, não da forma.)
- What I'm trying to say is it's not easy. (O que estou tentando dizer é que não é fácil.)

---

**2. "Could you…?" — pedidos educados para esclarecimento**

> Could you say that differently?

"Could you" é uma forma educada de pedir algo. Muito usado para pedir esclarecimento:
- Could you repeat that? (Você poderia repetir?)
- Could you explain what you mean? (Você poderia explicar o que quer dizer?)
- Could you speak more slowly? (Você poderia falar mais devagar?)
- Could you give me an example? (Você poderia me dar um exemplo?)

---

**3. "I didn't mean it like that" — negando uma interpretação errada**

> I didn't mean it like that!

Estrutura para dizer que alguém entendeu errado o que você quis dizer:
- I didn't mean it like that. (Não quis dizer assim.)
- I didn't mean to be rude. (Não quis ser grosso.)
- That's not what I meant. (Não foi isso que eu quis dizer.)
- Sorry, that came out wrong. (Desculpa, saiu errado.)

---

### 🌍 Contexto cultural

- **"Piece of cake"** é uma expressão muito comum nos EUA que significa "muito fácil." Crianças americanas usam bastante. Não tem nada a ver com bolo de verdade!
- **Pen pals** (amigos por correspondência) são muito populares entre crianças americanas. Hoje em dia, a maioria se comunica por videochamada em vez de cartas.
- **Baseball** é o esporte nacional dos EUA. Muitas expressões do dia a dia vêm do beisebol, como "home run" (grande sucesso) e "strike out" (falhar).
- Nos EUA, quando alguém não entende algo, é muito normal e educado dizer **"Sorry, I didn't catch that"** ou **"Could you say that again?"** — ninguém fica ofendido. É considerado melhor pedir esclarecimento do que fingir que entendeu.
