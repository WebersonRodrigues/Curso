# Texto 2 – Now I Can Speak English!

## 📖 Texto – "Now I Can Speak English!"

Dear Diary,

I can't believe how far I've come. **Looking back,** I remember when English was just a bunch of weird sounds that made no sense to me.

When I first started learning, **I was so scared** of making mistakes. I'd sit in class thinking: "What if I say something wrong and everyone laughs?" **To be honest,** I barely opened my mouth for the first few weeks.

I remember trying to watch cartoons in English and **not understanding a single word.** I'd just stare at the screen, feeling frustrated. "**I'll never get this,**" I thought.

But **little by little,** things started to change. First, I learned to say simple things — "hi," "how are you," "I like pizza." It wasn't much, **but it was a start.**

Then I started listening every single day. Songs, videos, podcasts — anything I could find. **At first,** I only caught a few words here and there. But **over time,** my ears got used to the sounds.

**The biggest thing** that helped me was **not being afraid of mistakes.** I used to think I had to be perfect. Now I know that's not true. **Mistakes are how you learn.**

I remember the first time I talked to my pen pal, Emma, on a video call. My hands were shaking! I said "I have twelve years" instead of "I'm twelve years old." She smiled and corrected me gently. **No big deal.**

Now? I can have **real conversations** with her. We talk about school, movies, music — **you name it.** Sometimes I don't know a word, and I just say: "**How do I say this?**" or "**What's the word for…?**" And that's totally fine.

Last month, something amazing happened. A tourist couple stopped me on the street and asked for directions. **And I actually helped them!** In English! They said: "Wow, your English is great!" **I was so proud.**

**I'm not gonna lie** — there are still things I struggle with. Phrasal verbs confuse me sometimes, and I still mix up "make" and "do." **But you know what?** That's okay.

**Here's what I've learned** about learning English: you don't need to be perfect. You just need to be brave enough to try. Listen every day, even if it's just five minutes. Don't be afraid to sound silly. Practice with friends. And **most importantly** — enjoy it!

English isn't just a school subject for me anymore. It's a **superpower.** I can understand songs, watch videos without subtitles, read cool stuff online, and talk to people from all over the world.

**I'm not perfect, but I can communicate. And that's what matters!**

So yeah, diary — **I did it.** I can speak English. And I'm just getting started.

### 🧠 Estruturas gramaticais do texto

**1. "I used to…" — algo que você fazia antes mas não faz mais**

> I used to think I had to be perfect.

"Used to" descreve hábitos ou situações do passado que mudaram:
- I used to be scared of English. (Eu costumava ter medo de inglês.)
- I used to watch cartoons in Portuguese. (Eu costumava assistir desenhos em português.)
- She used to live in São Paulo. (Ela costumava morar em São Paulo.)

Diferença: "I used to think" = eu pensava antes (mas não penso mais).

---

**2. "I'd + verbo" — passado habitual (would)**

> I'd sit in class thinking…  
> I'd just stare at the screen.

"I'd" aqui = "I would" para descrever algo que você fazia repetidamente no passado:
- I'd wake up early every day. (Eu acordava cedo todo dia.)
- I'd try to understand but couldn't. (Eu tentava entender mas não conseguia.)
- We'd play soccer after school. (A gente jogava futebol depois da aula.)

Parecido com "used to," mas "would" é mais para ações repetidas, não estados.

---

**3. Present Perfect para conquistas — "I've come" / "I've learned"**

> I can't believe how far I've come.  
> Here's what I've learned.

Present Perfect (have/has + particípio) para falar de conquistas até agora:
- I've improved a lot. (Eu melhorei muito.)
- I've learned so many words. (Aprendi tantas palavras.)
- She's become fluent. (Ela se tornou fluente.)

Conecta o passado ao presente: "comecei antes e o resultado está aqui agora."

---

### 🌍 Contexto cultural

- **Pen pals** (amigos por correspondência) são uma tradição antiga nos EUA. Hoje em dia, crianças se conectam por videochamada e mensagens, mas o conceito é o mesmo: fazer amizade com alguém de outro país para praticar idiomas e aprender sobre outras culturas.
- Nos EUA, o erro "I have twelve years" (em vez de "I'm twelve years old") é um dos mais comuns entre falantes de português e espanhol. Americanos acham fofo e geralmente corrigem com gentileza. Ninguém ri de quem está aprendendo.
- A ideia de que **"mistakes are how you learn"** é muito valorizada na cultura educacional americana. Professores nos EUA frequentemente dizem: "There are no stupid questions" (não existem perguntas burras) e encorajam alunos a arriscar.
- **Ajudar turistas** é uma experiência muito comum para jovens brasileiros que aprendem inglês, especialmente em cidades turísticas. É uma das primeiras situações reais onde o inglês aprendido na escola se torna útil de verdade.
