# Texto 2 – The New Student

## 📖 Texto – "The New Student"

Last September, something interesting happened at my school — a new student arrived from Japan, and his name was Kenji.

On his first day, our teacher said: "Class, I want to **introduce** you to Kenji. He is from Tokyo, and this is his first time in Brazil."

Everyone looked at him. He seemed very **shy** and didn't say much. I noticed that he had a small **accent** when he spoke English.

Some kids whispered: "He talks **different**." But I thought his accent was actually really cool.

At lunchtime, Kenji sat alone at a table. I felt bad because I remembered how scary it is to be new somewhere.

I decided to **include** him, so I walked over and said: "Hi! Do you want to sit with us?"

He looked surprised but smiled and said: "Yes, thank you!" I could tell he was happy that someone talked to him.

During lunch, I was **curious** about his life in Japan. I asked: "What is school like in Tokyo?"

He told me about his **culture** and **traditions**. In Japan, students clean their own classrooms every day!

I was surprised because that is very **different** from our school. However, some things were **similar** — he also loved soccer and video games, just like me.

Even though Kenji spoke English well, he sometimes had to **struggle** with difficult words.

Once, he couldn't remember the word for "borracha" in English. I helped him and said: "It's called an eraser!"

He laughed and taught me the Japanese word: "keshigomu." It was fun to **translate** words for each other.

After a few weeks, Kenji started to **adapt** to our school. He made more friends and talked more in class.

One day, he brought Japanese snacks for everyone. He explained that sharing food is an important **tradition** in his country.

The snacks were delicious! Some had flavors that were very **different** from what we eat in Brazil, but everyone loved them.

Kenji also taught us how to write our names in Japanese characters. My name looked so cool in Japanese!

I noticed that even though we came from **different** countries, we had many **similar** interests and feelings.

He told me: "I was scared to come here because everything was **foreign** to me. But you made me feel **welcome**."

That made me feel really good inside. I realized that a small act of kindness can change someone's whole experience.

Our **friendship** grew stronger every day. We learned from each other — I taught him Portuguese words, and he taught me Japanese.

Even though we had **different** backgrounds, our **friendship** showed me that people are more **similar** than we think.

By the end of the year, Kenji was one of the most popular kids in class. Everyone loved his stories about Japan.

I learned something important: when someone is new and **struggling** to **adapt**, all they need is one person to be kind.

Being **curious** about other **cultures** doesn't just help others — it makes your own life richer and more interesting.

Our teacher said: "True **friendship** has no borders." And I think she was absolutely right.

## 🇧🇷 Tradução – "O Aluno Novo"

Decidi **incluí-lo**, então fui até ele e disse: "Oi! Quer sentar com a gente?"

Ele pareceu surpreso, mas sorriu e disse: "Sim, obrigado!" Dava pra perceber que ele ficou feliz que alguém falou com ele.

Durante o almoço, eu estava **curioso** sobre a vida dele no Japão. Perguntei: "Como é a escola em Tóquio?"

Uma vez, ele não conseguia lembrar a palavra pra "borracha" em inglês. Eu ajudei e disse: "Se chama eraser!"

Ele riu e me ensinou a palavra em japonês: "keshigomu." Era divertido **traduzir** palavras um pro outro.

Kenji também nos ensinou a escrever nossos nomes em caracteres japoneses. Meu nome ficou muito legal em japonês!

Nossa **amizade** ficou mais forte a cada dia. Aprendemos um com o outro — eu ensinei palavras em português pra ele, e ele me ensinou japonês.

Mesmo tendo origens **diferentes**, nossa **amizade** me mostrou que as pessoas são mais **parecidas** do que a gente pensa.

No final do ano, Kenji era um dos alunos mais populares da turma. Todo mundo adorava as histórias dele sobre o Japão.

Aprendi algo importante: quando alguém é novo e está **lutando** pra se **adaptar**, tudo que essa pessoa precisa é de alguém sendo gentil.

Ser **curioso** sobre outras **culturas** não ajuda só os outros — torna a sua própria vida mais rica e interessante.

Nossa professora disse: "A verdadeira **amizade** não tem fronteiras." E eu acho que ela estava completamente certa.

### 🧠 Estruturas gramaticais do texto

**1. "I noticed that..." / "I decided to..." — Verbos de percepção e decisão no passado**
> I noticed that he had a small accent.
> I decided to include him.

Usamos "noticed that" para contar algo que percebemos, e "decided to" para contar uma decisão:
- I noticed that she was sad. (Notei que ela estava triste.)
- I decided to help him. (Decidi ajudá-lo.)
- He noticed that everyone was looking at him. (Ele notou que todos estavam olhando pra ele.)

---

**2. "Even though... we..." — Concessão com contraste**
> Even though we came from different countries, we had many similar interests.
> Even though Kenji spoke English well, he sometimes struggled with difficult words.

Usamos "even though" para mostrar que algo é verdade APESAR de outra coisa:
- Even though he was shy, he smiled at me. (Mesmo sendo tímido, ele sorriu pra mim.)
- Even though we are different, we are friends. (Mesmo sendo diferentes, somos amigos.)
- Even though it was hard, he never gave up. (Mesmo sendo difícil, ele nunca desistiu.)

---

**3. "He told me about..." — Reported speech (discurso indireto)**
> He told me about his culture and traditions.
> He told me: "Everything was foreign to me."

Usamos "told me about" para contar o que alguém nos disse sobre um assunto, e "told me + frase" para citar o que disseram:
- She told me about her trip. (Ela me contou sobre a viagem dela.)
- My friend told me about his new school. (Meu amigo me contou sobre a escola nova dele.)
- He told me that he missed his country. (Ele me disse que sentia falta do país dele.)

### 🌍 Contexto cultural

- No Japão, os alunos realmente limpam suas salas de aula, corredores e banheiros da escola todos os dias. Isso ensina responsabilidade e respeito pelo espaço coletivo — um valor cultural chamado "souji" (掃除).
- Compartilhar comida é muito importante na cultura japonesa. Levar presentes (chamados "omiyage") quando visita alguém ou chega a um lugar novo é uma tradição de respeito e gratidão.
- Nos EUA e no Brasil, escolas recebem muitos alunos de outros países. Programas de "buddy system" (sistema de amigos) ajudam alunos novos a se adaptarem, colocando um colega local para acompanhá-los nos primeiros dias.
- A palavra "foreign" pode soar negativa em alguns contextos. Em inglês moderno, muitas pessoas preferem dizer "international student" (aluno internacional) em vez de "foreign student" (aluno estrangeiro), porque soa mais acolhedor e respeitoso.
