# Texto 1 – The Alphabet

## 📖 Texto – "The Alphabet"

Today my teacher **taught** us the English alphabet. It was so cool!

I already **know** the alphabet in Portuguese, but in English the letters **sound** very different. My teacher **said**: "The English alphabet has 26 letters, just like Portuguese. But the sounds are not the same!"

Then she **asked** us to repeat after her. We all **said** the letters together. It was fun, like singing a song!

After that, my teacher **explained** the tricky letters — the ones that confuse Brazilians.

She **started** with the letter **A**. In Portuguese, A sounds like "ah". But in English, A sounds like "ei"! So when you **spell** a word with A, you say "ei". That is very different!

Then she **said**: "Now look at the letter **E**." In Portuguese, E sounds like "eh". But in English, E sounds like "i"! I **thought** that was so confusing. E sounds like our I!

And the letter **I**? In English, I sounds like "ai"! Not like our "i" at all. My friend Lis **laughed** and **said**: "So E sounds like I, and I sounds like ai? That's crazy!"

My teacher **smiled** and **said**: "I know! But you will get used to it."

Then she **showed** us the letter **H**. In Portuguese, H is silent — it has no sound. But in English, H sounds like "eitch"! And when you say words like "house" or "happy", you can **hear** the H sound.

The letter **R** was funny too. In English, R sounds like "ar". Very different from our "erre". My teacher **said** the English R is softer.

Then she **talked** about **W**. She **said**: "This letter is called double-u. We don't use W much in Portuguese, but in English it is very common." Words like "water", "window", and "week" all start with W.

The last tricky letter was **Y**. In English, Y sounds like "uai". My teacher **explained** that Y can be a vowel or a consonant. In "yes", it is a consonant. In "happy", it is a vowel. That was interesting!

After learning the tricky letters, my teacher **asked** me: "Can you **spell** your name?"

I **thought** for a moment. My name is Sofia. So I **said**: "S - O - F - I - A."

She **smiled** and **said**: "Perfect! Now you can spell your name in English!"

Then my friend Lis **tried** too. She **said**: "L - I - S."

And Ana **said**: "A - N - A."

My teacher **clapped** and **said**: "Great job, everyone!"

She also **taught** us a useful phrase: "How do you **spell** that?"

We use this when we don't know how to write a word. For example:

"My name is Sofia."
"How do you spell that?"
"S - O - F - I - A."

I **think** the English alphabet is fun. The sounds are different, but with practice, it gets easier.

Now I can **spell** my name, my city, and even my favorite food: P - I - Z - Z - A!

My mom **asked** me at home: "What did you learn today?"

I **answered**: "I learned the English alphabet! The letters sound so different! Did you know that E sounds like 'i' and I sounds like 'ai'?"

She **laughed** and **said**: "That is confusing! But I'm sure you will learn it."

And I will! With practice, everything gets easier.

### 🧠 Estruturas gramaticais do texto

**1. "How do you spell…?" — como você soletra…?**
> How do you spell that? / How do you spell your name?

Pergunta essencial para quando você não sabe escrever uma palavra:
- How do you spell "beautiful"? (Como soletra "beautiful"?)
- Can you spell that for me? (Pode soletrar pra mim?)

---

**2. "It sounds like…" — soa como…**
> The letter A sounds like "ei".

Estrutura para descrever sons e pronúncias:
- It sounds like "cat". (Soa como "cat".)
- That sounds different. (Isso soa diferente.)
- It sounds easy! (Parece fácil!)

---

**3. "Can you + verbo?" — você consegue…?**
> Can you spell your name?

Forma de perguntar sobre habilidade:
- Can you say the alphabet? (Você consegue falar o alfabeto?)
- Can you repeat that? (Pode repetir?)
- Can you hear the difference? (Consegue ouvir a diferença?)

---

**4. Passado irregular: "taught" (ensinou)**
> My teacher taught us the alphabet.

"Teach" (ensinar) tem passado irregular "taught":
- She taught me English. (Ela me ensinou inglês.)
- Who taught you that? (Quem te ensinou isso?)

---

**5. "You will get used to it" — você vai se acostumar**
> I know! But you will get used to it.

Estrutura para dizer que algo vai ficar natural com o tempo:
- You will get used to the sounds. (Você vai se acostumar com os sons.)
- I'm getting used to English. (Estou me acostumando com inglês.)

---

**6. "With practice, it gets easier" — com prática, fica mais fácil**
> The sounds are different, but with practice, it gets easier.

Estrutura motivacional muito usada:
- With time, you will improve. (Com tempo, você vai melhorar.)
- With practice, everything gets better. (Com prática, tudo melhora.)

---

### 🌍 Contexto cultural

- Em inglês, **soletrar (spelling)** é muito importante. Americanos soletram nomes, endereços e palavras o tempo todo no dia a dia.
- Nos EUA existe até uma competição famosa chamada **"Spelling Bee"** onde crianças competem soletrando palavras difíceis.
- A letra **Z** é pronunciada "zi" nos EUA e "zed" no Reino Unido e Canadá.
- **"Double-u" (W)** se chama assim porque antigamente era escrita como dois "U" juntos (UU).
- Quando alguém não entende seu nome, a frase mais comum é **"Can you spell that for me?"** (Pode soletrar pra mim?).
- As letras **A, E, I** são as que mais confundem brasileiros porque os sons são "trocados" em relação ao português.
